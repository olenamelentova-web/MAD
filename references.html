How This Course Was Built

References

An honest account of what shaped this course's design — not a list of sources that were copied from, but the general teaching principles and public resources it draws on.

✎ A note on how this was made

All 30 lessons — the vocabulary, dialogues, grammar explanations, and exercises — were written specifically for Yaroslava's situation, using Claude (an AI assistant by Anthropic). Nothing here is copied from a textbook or existing course. What follows are the general, publicly known teaching principles and free resources that informed the pedagogical structure, offered for transparency and for anyone who wants to dig deeper into the "why" behind the design choices.

📐 Frameworks referenced for level and structure

  • CEFR — Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (Council of Europe). Used as the general reference point for what "A1," "A2," and "B1" mean, and for sequencing grammar and topics in a roughly appropriate order of difficulty.
  • Cambridge English Qualifications (A2 Key, B1 Preliminary). Used loosely as a sense-check for what vocabulary and functional language is realistic at each stage — not followed as a syllabus.

🧭 Teaching approaches behind the design

  • Communicative Language Teaching (CLT) — the general approach of prioritizing real communication and fluency over grammatical perfection, which is why every lesson leads with speaking, not grammar explanation.
  • Task-Based Language Teaching (TBLT) — the principle of building lessons around real-world tasks (ordering food, asking for directions, making a phone call) rather than abstract grammar points in isolation.
  • Comprehensible input (associated with linguist Stephen Krashen) — the general idea that learners acquire language most effectively through meaningful, understandable exposure, which shaped the decision to keep grammar explanations minimal and speaking practice extensive.
  • Spaced repetition — the general principle that revisiting material at increasing intervals (rather than once) builds retention, which is why vocabulary and grammar from earlier lessons resurface in every unit review and in Lesson 30's final mix.

🔗 Free public resources worth knowing about

Not sources this course copied from — genuinely useful, freely available platforms for anyone teaching or learning English.

  • British Council LearnEnglish — free lessons, listening, and grammar reference organized by CEFR level
  • BBC Learning English — free listening practice, including "6 Minute English" and news-based lessons
  • Cambridge English — official information on exam-level expectations and free practice materials

✎ In short

  • The content (every word, dialogue, and exercise) is original, written for this specific learner and her specific situations.
  • The structure (grammar-first warm-ups, speaking-heavy practice, spaced review lessons) draws on standard, widely taught ELT principles rather than any single proprietary curriculum.
  • If you're building your own course, the resources above are a reasonable place to start reading further.
